If you've ever walked into a music store and felt a little overwhelmed by the sheer variety of pianos, you're not alone. Pianos come in all shapes and sizes — and they don't all behave the same way when it comes time to tune them.

The piano has the largest range of any instrument — certainly any stringed one. That range comes with a quirk: the lower strings use copper winding to produce low pitches, but this creates **inharmonicity** — a fancy word for "slightly imperfect harmonic relationships."

A good tuner listens to what your piano is doing and tunes according to its own internal logic.

## The Upright Family

Uprights come in four sizes, categorized by height:

**Spinet (36"–40")** — The smallest of the bunch. Think of it as the iPod Nano of pianos. Compact and accessible, but limited in tone and projection. Great for tight spaces, but not ideal if you're looking for rich sound.

**Console (40"–44")** — The most common home piano you'll find. A solid middle ground between portability and sound quality. If you've got a piano in your living room, there's a good chance it's a console.

**Studio (45"–48")** — Now we're getting serious. Studios produce a fuller, more resonant sound than consoles. You'll find these in music schools, churches, and practice rooms. They're heavy enough that you'll want help moving one.

**Full Upright (50"–52")** — The top of the upright class. These tall instruments produce a surprisingly rich sound that can rival some smaller grands. If you've inherited a beautiful old upright with ornate woodwork, it's probably one of these.

## The Grand Lineup

Grand pianos come in six recognized sizes:

**Petite Grand** — Under 5 feet. The entry point to the grand piano world. Beautiful to look at, though the shorter string length means slightly less tonal depth.

**Baby Grand** — Around 5'5". The most popular grand piano size for homes. Strikes a great balance between visual presence and musical quality.

**Medium Grand** — Just under 6 feet. A noticeable step up in projection and clarity. Popular with serious hobbyists and small performance venues.

**Parlor Grand** — Weighs roughly 650 pounds. This is where the piano starts demanding its own room — and honestly, it deserves one.

**Semi-Concert Grand** — About 7 feet of piano. Used in small concert halls and recording studios. The tonal range and dynamic control are exceptional.

**Concert Grand** — 9 feet long. Tall enough to dunk a basketball. This is the instrument you see on the world's great stages. Steinway's Model D, Bösendorfer's Imperial — these are the legends.

## Why It All Matters for Tuning

Every piano type responds differently to tuning because of the relationship between string length, tension, and inharmonicity. A spinet's short strings behave very differently from a concert grand's long ones. The tuning approach has to account for the piano's specific geometry and voice.

Add in environmental factors — humidity and temperature swings here in Kansas City affect wood expansion and string tension — and you start to see why tuning isn't one-size-fits-all.
