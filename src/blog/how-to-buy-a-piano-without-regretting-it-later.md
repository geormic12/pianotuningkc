You've seen the listing. We all have.

**"Free Piano — Move it Yourself."**

It shows up on Facebook Marketplace like clockwork. The photo is slightly blurry, the piano is wedged into a corner next to a bookshelf and a cat tree, and the caption promises it "just needs a good tuning."

It's easy to find free pianos. It's much harder to find one worth moving.

## Nothing is Free

Piano movers in Kansas City typically charge $300 to $500. Tuning costs $150–$200. That's $500–$700 before you even play your first note. And if the piano turns out to need major repairs? You could be looking at thousands.

Pianos contain usually more than 10,000 moving parts. That's a lot of things that can go wrong — and a lot of things that can quietly be wrong without you noticing until it's too late.

## Brand Matters (But There's a Catch)

Some names carry serious weight: **Steinway & Sons, Bösendorfer, Mason & Hamlin, Baldwin, Yamaha, Kawai.** These are the brands that hold value and are worth investing in.

Then there's the secondary tier: **Samick, Petrof, Wurlitzer, Kimball.** Solid instruments, but they don't carry the same resale value or long-term reputation.

Here's the catch: **a piano is only worth what someone's willing to pay for it.** A Steinway that's been sitting in a flooded basement isn't worth more than a well-maintained Yamaha in a climate-controlled living room. Brand is a starting point — not a verdict.

## The Heart of the Piano

The soundboard is the large wooden plate (usually spruce) that amplifies the vibrations from the strings. It's the voice of the piano. Small cracks are normal in older pianos, but wide separations or gaps are signs of structural fatigue. If the soundboard is compromised, the piano will never sound right — no matter how well you tune it.

## The Feel Test

Sit down and play every single key. Yes, all 88. You're listening and feeling for:

- Keys that stick or don't return
- Clicks, clunks, or buzzing
- Uneven touch — some keys heavy, some light
- Dead spots where the sound just drops out

## Hammers and Strings

Open the top and look at the hammers — they're the felt-covered pieces that strike the strings. Deep grooves in the felt indicate heavy use. Grooves can be filed down (called "voicing"), but only so many times before the felt needs to be replaced entirely.

Check the strings for rust. Rust indicates humidity damage, which often means the rest of the piano has been affected too.

## Quick Piano Evaluation Checklist

- ✅ Inspect brand and serial number (look it up online for age)
- ✅ Look at soundboard for cracks or separation
- ✅ Play every key — listen and feel
- ✅ Check hammer felt for deep grooves
- ✅ Press each pedal — all should function smoothly
- ✅ Examine strings and tuning pins for rust
- ✅ Check cabinet for water stains, warping, or mold
- ✅ Avoid spinets unless sentimental
- ✅ Trust your ears — does it sound pleasant?

## Final Thoughts

Buying a used or free piano doesn't have to be a gamble — it just takes a little homework. Knowing what to look for can save you hundreds (or thousands) in surprise repairs.
